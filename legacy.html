Betrayal

21st June 1582

By 1582 Oda Nobunaga was at the height of his power and essentially the ruler of Japan. One of Nobunagas last remaining enemies, the Mori clan, were at threat of losing Takamatsu Castle, a vital stronghold to the Mori which would have led the way to capturing the provincial capital. Hashiba Hideyoshi, Nobunaga's general laying siege to the castle, requested reinforcements to take the castle. Nobunaga ordered several of his leading generals to prepare their armies which he himself would lead. Nobunaga left Azuchi castle to head his army, but on the way he would hold a tea ceremony at Honno-ji temple.

At the temple Nobunaga was ambushed by Akechi Mitsuhide who had decided to assasinate him for unknown reasons. Nobunaga and his 30 guards were quickly overrun and Nobunaga retreated to one of the inner rooms to commit seppuke, ritual sacrifice. Mori Ranmaru, Nobunagas last remaining guard, set fire to the temple whilst still inside, in accordance with his masters last orders, so that his body could not be decapitated by his enemies. However Nobunagas body was never recovered, a fact which still draws discussion from historians today.

Nobunaga was succeeded by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, one of Nobunagas greatest generals. Hideyoshi would go on to pacify and maintain control of the Oda territory and eventually expand this territory, further creating a single, central Japan. Toyotomi Hideyoshi is known as the second great unifier of Japan.

Unification of Japan

1568 - 1582

After installing Yoshiaki as shogun, Nobunaga had evidently pressed Yoshiaki to request all the local daimyō to come to Kyoto and attend a certain banquet. Asakura Yoshikage, head of the Asakura clan and regent of Ashikaga Yoshiaki, refused, an act Nobunaga declared disloyal to both the shogun and the emperor. With this pretext well in hand, Nobunaga raised an army and marched on Echizen. In early 1570, Nobunaga launched a campaign into the Asakura clan's domain and besieged Kanagasaki Castle. This action made a conflict between Nobunaga and shogun Ashikaga Yoshiaki, as their relationship grew difficult, Yoshiaki secretly started an "anti-Nobunaga alliance", conspiring with other daimyō to get rid of Nobunaga. Azai Nagamasa, to whom Nobunaga's sister Oichi was married, broke the alliance with the Oda clan to honor the Azai-Asakura alliance, which had lasted for generations. With the help of the Rokkaku clan, Miyoshi clan, and the Ikkō-ikki, the anti-Nobunaga alliance sprang into full force, taking a heavy toll on the Oda clan.

The "anti-Nobunaga alliance" continued to operate and draw allies against Oda Nobunaga. This would result in some of the bloodiest fighting ever seen in feudal Japan. Over the next 14 years Nobunaga campaigned, depending existing territory and conquered new territory. In 1578 the Imperial Court, following a string of court appointments to placate Nobunaga, granted him the title of Grand Minister of State, the highest post that could be given. In 1579 the construction of Azuchi castle was completed. Nobunaga built this castle in its strategic location close to Kyoto, so that he guard the approaches to the capital city. Nobunaga is known for having great respect for the emperor and publicly expressed his support for the Imperial system, however historians also believe that the construction of Azuchi castle, with its proximity to Kyoto, was a statement by Nobunaga. He provided the defense, but he also held the real power and control of Japan and was in many ways its de facto ruler.
