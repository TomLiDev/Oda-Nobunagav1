Betrayal

21st June 1582

By 1582 Oda Nobunaga was at the height of his power and essentially the ruler of Japan. One of Nobunagas last remaining enemies, the Mori clan, were at threat of losing Takamatsu Castle, a vital stronghold to the Mori which would have led the way to capturing the provincial capital. Hashiba Hideyoshi, Nobunaga's general laying siege to the castle, requested reinforcements to take the castle. Nobunaga ordered several of his leading generals to prepare their armies which he himself would lead. Nobunaga left Azuchi castle to head his army, but on the way he would hold a tea ceremony at Honno-ji temple.

At the temple Nobunaga was ambushed by Akechi Mitsuhide who had decided to assasinate him for unknown reasons. Nobunaga and his 30 guards were quickly overrun and Nobunaga retreated to one of the inner rooms to commit seppuke, ritual sacrifice. Mori Ranmaru, Nobunagas last remaining guard, set fire to the temple whilst still inside, in accordance with his masters last orders, so that his body could not be decapitated by his enemies. However Nobunagas body was never recovered, a fact which still draws discussion from historians today.

Nobunaga was succeeded by Toyotomi Hideyoshi, one of Nobunagas greatest generals. Hideyoshi would go on to pacify and maintain control of the Oda territory and eventually expand this territory, further creating a single, central Japan. Toyotomi Hideyoshi is known as the second great unifier of Japan.

History and Legacy

Ongoing

The goal of national unification and a return to the comparative political stability of the earlier Muromachi period was widely shared by the multitude of autonomous daimyō during the Sengoku period. Oda Nobunaga was the first for whom this goal seemed attainable. Nobunaga had gained control over most of Honshu before his death during the 1582 Honnō-ji incident, a coup attempt executed by Nobunaga's vassal, Akechi Mitsuhide.

Nobunaga's successful subjugation of much of Honshu enabled the later successes of his allies Hideyoshi and Tokugawa Ieyasu toward the goal of national unification by subjugating local daimyō under a hereditary shogunate, which was ultimately accomplished in 1603 when Ieyasu (The Third Great Unifier of Japan) was granted the title of Shogun by Emperor Go-Yōzei following the successful Sekigahara Campaign of 1600. The nature of the succession of power through the three daimyō is reflected in a well-known Japanese idiom:
Nobunaga pounds the national rice cake, Hideyoshi kneads it, and in the end, Ieyasu sits down and eats it.
